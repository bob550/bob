CHAPTER XIX
Robin woke from a heavy slumber at daybreak. A faint noise from without the buttery
disturbed him. He very quietly rose up, and, picking his way across the room, came to the
entrance to the kitchens. He opened one of the doors and found a passage, grey-lit by the
first gleam of dawn.
At the end of it was the figure of a man. His height revealed him for Little John. Over his
shoulders was a short sack.
Seeing Robin, he beckoned to him; then whispered his plans. But Robin did not intend to
leave Nottingham so soon.
"Go, Little John, and take that which is in your sack——"
"I shall bring it to you, gossip," spoke Little John, in a muffled voice: "to your haunts in
Bar nesdale. You shall see who is the better servant—Stuteley or myself. Here have I the
Sheriff 's plate——"
An audacious notion flashed upon Robin.
"Take it to our cave in Barnesdale, honest John," said he, swiftly, indicating the sack,
"and, harkee; I will follow later with such a guest as never our greenwood has yet car ried.
