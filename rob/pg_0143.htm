Lif e passed uneventfully for them in the Fitzwalter household. It was neither happy nor
unhappy. Mistress Fennel found it vastly more amusing than the draughty caves of
Bar nesdale; but then Mistress Fennel had her dear—and Marian had not. She was

determined where he would live—Nottingham or London.
The months crawled on and Christmas came and went.
Marian was still tied to Nottingham streets and Robin to Barnesdale woods. This state of
inactivity had told much upon the greenwood men—upon Little John most of all.
At last the big fellow fell out with Friar Tuck, and began to grumble at everyone in turn.
Robin, in despair, bade him go into Nottingham, to see how the land lay there. "If you
must be breaking someone's head, Little John, let it be one of our enemies who shall
suf fer. But have a care, for your tongue is as long as your body. Choose a cunning
disguise therefor."
"I will go as a beggar," said Little John, brightening up at the prospect of adventure. "For
a beggar may chatter as much as he will—'tis part of his trade."
So clad all in rags, and bent double as though with age, Little John went forth from their
caves upon a February morning. He supported himself with a stout oak staff, and car ried
two great bags upon his shoulders. One held his food, and the other was to be refuge for
anything of note that he might find left about—such as Sheriff's plate, to wit, or a Bishop's
valuables.

company. What is there a-doing in Nottingham since the bells be ringing a-merrily? Are
